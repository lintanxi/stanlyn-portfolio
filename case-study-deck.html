Warden
01
The situation I inherited

A platform that had outgrown its foundations

Warden was built fast. Growth from a handful of incident types to 72+ across 11 categories — more regions, more compliance, a designer transition — left a system never designed to be maintained at this scale. I joined as the sole designer on a ~40-person team.

Operational Complexity
72+ incident types, 11+ categories, 80+ question categories, a taxonomy still actively evolving. Regional and compliance rules with no unified logic.
UX Debt
A codebase mixing Material UI, Ant Design, and custom styling. Inconsistent patterns across surfaces; a prior sub-system only partially adopted.
Governance Bottleneck
Every questionnaire change required a spreadsheet, approval meetings, an engineering ticket, and a release cycle. Needs moved faster than the system could adapt.
Solo Coverage
As the only designer: new feature design, design-system alignment across three layers, and AI evaluation — with no handover from the previous designer.

02
Stakeholder Research

Three different problems. One root cause.

I interviewed three stakeholders with different vantage points — operations, the questionnaire content owner, and the PM who bore the cost of every change.

Rick
Director, Operations & Technology · GSOC
"When something changes operationally, we need to update the questionnaire quickly — but by the time a change reaches operators, the situation has moved on."
Lizzy

"I maintain a huge spreadsheet of questions, and every change requires reviews and approvals from multiple leaders before anything goes to engineering."
Mike
Product Manager · Warden
"Every questionnaire change becomes an engineering effort. We need to reduce friction and manual work without creating compliance or data risk."
Warden
03
Discovery · The reframe

The real problem was workflow governance, not the workflow

A single questionnaire change followed this path: spreadsheet → approval meetings → engineering ticket → release cycle. Weeks, for what was essentially a content edit.

Worse, a simple change had downstream effects on compliance reporting, dashboards, and notification subscriptions — none visible to the person requesting it. That invisibility was the real problem.

Cross-interview insight

Workflow ownership was trapped in spreadsheets, approvals, and release cycles. The people who understood what needed to change had no path to change it.

Warden
04
0→1 Design

Designing the boundary between flexibility and governance

The central question was what to allow, not just what to show. Phase 1 gave Super Admins four configurable dimensions:

  • Visibility — show/hide per question
  • Ordering — drag-to-reorder
  • Requiredness — required vs. optional
  • Country rules — region-based logic

Every change is logged to a persistent audit trail — surfaced in the view, not buried in settings. Auditability was a first-class requirement.

Warden
05
Design Strategy · The hardest decision

Earning trust before expanding capability

The most important decision was what not to build yet. Question authoring was excluded from Phase 1 — not for technical reasons, but because the taxonomy was mid-consolidation and premature access would have created real compliance exposure.

✕ Rejected — Full authoring on day one

Maximum flexibility, but the taxonomy was mid-consolidation, approvals weren't established, and question changes cascade into compliance reports, dashboards, and subscription logic.

✓ Chosen — Configuration first, authoring later

Phase 1 introduces real operational control (visibility, ordering, requiredness) while deferring authoring until governance matures. Content stays stable; only behavior is configurable.

Phase 1 · Shipped
Show/hide · reorder · required toggle · country rules · compliance locks · full audit trail
Upcoming phases
Create/edit/remove questions · sub-question logic · category management · versioning & rollback · AI-assisted config
Warden
06
Scope Beyond the Platform

Evaluating AI in high-stakes workflows

AI-assisted incident reporting

Store managers describe a situation in plain language; the AI pre-populates the form as a draft — every field editable, ambiguities flagged for confirmation. The AI never submits on their behalf.

AI-assisted incident summary

Operators managing concurrent incidents can surface a one-paragraph summary on demand — enough to assess severity and act. Always on demand, never auto-shown.

Design Principle

AI assists documentation. Humans remain accountable.

The risk in a high-stakes workflow isn't AI extracting data wrong — it's an operator trusting output they didn't verify. Every pattern (disambiguation prompts, editable drafts, accuracy reminders) was designed around that.

Impact & Reflection

What was delivered — and what I'd do differently

0→1
Super Admin configuration platform — no comparable tooling existed before
3
surfaces aligned under a unified governance & design-system strategy for the first time
∞→1
design-system layers reduced to a single documented alignment strategy with LD 3.5

Honest note: success measures are directional — there was almost no baseline data. If I did it again, I'd push for instrumentation from day one.

The most valuable outcome was the governance framework itself: a clear boundary between what's configurable and what needs engineering, an audit trail as accountability, and a phased model that builds trust before expanding authority.

Twelve campuses, twelve different check-in processes — fragmented, and none designed with accessibility in mind. I unified them into one accessible service.

Visit Check-in
01
My Role & the constraints

Sole lead designer, end to end

I owned every surface — kiosk, preregistration web flow, email system, and admin portal — and led the accessibility strategy. WCAG 2.1 AA shaped the interaction model from day one.

Hardware
Surface Go's compact screen — every interaction one decision deep.
Connectivity
Unreliable at several sites — full offline mode with paper fallbacks.
Inclusivity
Motion sensitivity, mount heights, screen-reader support — designed in.
Fragmentation
Twelve implementations, no shared library, no cross-campus visibility.
Accessibility gap: for some visitors, check-in simply didn't work — no keyboard path, no contrast compliance. A non-negotiable constraint from day one.
Visit Check-in
02
Discovery

The problem the software couldn't see

Every implementation was built inside-out — what does the system need to capture? Nobody asked what the visitor, host, or front-desk associate needed to do. The screens were fine; the service around them had no connective tissue.

"I end up re-entering half the visitors myself because the screen times out on them. None of that shows up anywhere."— Front-desk associate, field interview
1 · Preregistration
The highest-leverage fix nobody had prioritized — the tool existed in a separate system nobody knew about.
2 · Accessibility
Failures created invisible operational load — associates completing check-in for visitors who couldn't. The workaround had become the process.
3 · Host awareness
Hosts had no real-time signal their visitor arrived. Visitors waited in the lobby; no system solved it.
Visit Check-in
03
The hard part

Getting everyone to agree was the real design challenge

Each campus had a different definition of "welcoming," and sequential reviews stalled progress. Leadership engaged with visuals more readily than with service flows — so the landing page became a proxy for confidence in the entire system.

I recognized what was happening: stakeholders hadn't built a shared mental model, so the landing page became where anxiety surfaced.

What I changed

Instead of sequential reviews, I presented all directions simultaneously — video, carousel, static photography — each with explicit tradeoffs tied to goals we'd already aligned on. Making the decision space visible let stakeholders converge without feeling overruled.

Visit Check-in
04
The Design

Unified UI, local logic — one system, genuinely different campuses

Walk-in visitor journey: the opportunities row drove every kiosk screen — each traces to a specific field observation.

The answer was a configurable flow architecture: the same interface, with location-aware steps that activate by facility type.

  • NDA signing for California
  • Device registration for India (IDC)
  • Streamlined short-form for 8th & Plate

All on one codebase, one Surface Go. Walk-in kiosk operable in under 60 seconds without instruction — progressive disclosure, one decision per screen.

Visit Check-in
05
Closing the loops

Three capabilities the legacy system never had

Check-out & multi-building
Visitors scan at each entry/exit — no repeated check-ins. Every scan syncs in real time to the Visit Admin portal: a complete, time-stamped record of campus movement. Net-new visibility.
Preregistration
The tool existed but nobody knew. I wired the prereg link directly into the host's calendar invite — so sending the invite and the prep link became a single action. Pre-registered visitors clear check-in far faster.
Host notification
Any completed check-in triggers an instant alert to the host — defaulting to no action needed. One tap to decline; the front desk is then notified automatically. No phone call, no manual escalation.
Visit Check-in
06
Accessibility built into the practice

Keyboard-first, validated before handoff

  • 1

    Built the interaction model keyboard-navigation first — every touch interaction had a keyboard equivalent from day one.

  • 2

    Every screen validated against a shared accessibility checklist before it went to engineering.

  • 3

    Annotations embedded directly in Figma — contrast ratios, focus order, screen-reader labels — so developers had zero ambiguity at handoff.

Why it matters

A wheelchair user at the Bentonville turnstile had no guaranteed accessible check-in path. A security associate had flagged this gap for over a year before design started.

The operational backbone · Visit Admin

Behind the experience is the tool security runs on

Before
Phase 1
Watchlist: icon-only threat flags → semantic labeled badges (Caution / Critical / Awareness). In a safety-critical module, ambiguity has direct consequences.

When engineering capacity shifted to the kiosk rebuild, I scoped Phase 1 to LD 3.5 migration + targeted usability fixes and kept the IA work documented and queued.

Phasing also de-risked adoption: many associates are 20+ year employees on the legacy manual process. Incremental releases let phasing read as strategy, not compromise.

09
Reflection

What I'd do differently

  • 1

    I'd instrument the service earlier. We had almost no baseline data — check-in times, prereg rates, accessibility escalations were all unmeasured. Measurement should have been scoped in from the start, not advocated for mid-project.

  • 2

    I'd push harder for pilot-then-scale. We launched across all US campuses nearly simultaneously. A single high-traffic pilot with a six-week learning period would have surfaced group-booking edge cases — and saved two engineering sprints.

  • 3

    I underestimated the front-desk associate as a design partner. I interviewed them early but treated them as research subjects. I should have brought two in as co-reviewers — they caught workflow gaps I was too close to see.
